An independent research and data project

Where you list for a transplant
shapes your odds. Dramatically.

Wait times for kidneys, livers, hearts, and lungs vary enormously depending on where you're listed. Most patients never see this data compared side by side. Most researchers can't easily model how policy changes would shift these outcomes. This project tackles both.

This is an open-source project built on public data. If you're a developer, researcher, or transplant advocate, we'd love your help making it better.

The Landscape

248 transplant programs. One country.

Each marker is a hospital that performs organ transplants: kidneys, livers, hearts, lungs, pancreas, and intestine. Together they serve over 100,000 patients on the national waitlist.

They cluster around major metros. But patients come from everywhere, including rural areas hundreds of miles from the nearest center.

The Problem

They are not created equal.

Wait times at these centers vary by orders of magnitude, even for the same organ and blood type. Two hospitals 40 miles apart can differ by years.

Organ Supply

Traumatic deaths are a major source of donor organs.

A large share of transplantable organs come from sudden fatalities, particularly motor vehicle accidents. The shaded red regions show areas with higher traffic fatality rates.

These areas often don't overlap with major transplant centers, creating a geographic mismatch between where organs become available and where patients are waiting.

Fatality data from NHTSA via CDC WISQARS

Not All Donors Are Equal

Different causes of death preserve different organs.

Brain death from trauma (car accidents, falls) tends to preserve kidneys, livers, and pancreases well because blood flow continues. Cardiac death, by contrast, limits viability for hearts and lungs due to warm ischemia time.

This means a region's type of fatalities shapes which organs are available there, not just how many. Rural areas with high traffic fatalities may have strong kidney supply but limited heart availability.

We model this by weighting organ-specific donor rates per region, not just raw fatality counts.

Donor Willingness

Donor registration rates vary wildly by state.

How many people in a region have opted in as organ donors directly affects the supply pipeline. Blue shaded regions show higher registration density.

When you layer supply patterns, registration rates, hospital volume, and wait times, you start to see why where you list matters as much as your clinical profile.

The Methods

Monte Carlo simulation with competing risks.

For each center, we run 1,000 Monte Carlo iterations that model the probability of transplant at 6, 12, 24, and 36 months. Each iteration accounts for competing risks: transplant, mortality on the waitlist, and removal for other reasons.

A 48-profile demographic matrix evaluates equity across blood types, age brackets, and sex. The Gini coefficient measures how fairly each center distributes outcomes across patient profiles.

What-if scenarios let researchers adjust donor rates and wait-time multipliers to model how UNOS policy changes would shift transplant probabilities in real time.
6 / 8
The Composite

50+ variables. 8 weighted categories. One score.

transplant.today combines all of these layers into a composite suitability score: medical compatibility, wait times, donor availability, hospital quality (CMS), logistics, health demographics (CDC), air quality (EPA), and socioeconomic support (BLS).

Larger, brighter markers indicate higher composite scores. The weights adapt to your organ type, blood type, and clinical profile.

This is the kind of multi-factor analysis that wealthy patients pay consultants to perform. We made it free and open source.

For Patients and Researchers

A tool for decisions. A platform for discovery.

For patients: the Simulator gives you personalized center rankings with wait-time forecasts. The Center Explorer lets you compare programs side by side. No login, no cost.

For researchers: the Data tab exposes every layer as a toggleable overlay on the interactive map. Run what-if policy scenarios. Export results as CSV, JSON, or PDF. Explore how allocation policies, donor registration drives, and infrastructure investments could reshape equitable access to transplantation.

Free. Open source. No login required. Data refreshed weekly from 8 public sources.

Disclaimer

This tool is for educational and exploratory purposes only. transplant.today is not a medical device, does not provide medical advice, and cannot predict individual transplant outcomes.

Scores are relative comparisons based on publicly available regional statistics. They are not clinical assessments.

Always consult your transplant team and healthcare providers before making any decisions about where to list for a transplant.
